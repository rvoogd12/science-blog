Thermodynamics is the study of energy, heat, and how they move and change during any physical and chemical processes, like ice melting, wood burning, or water boiling.

## The Basics: System and Surroundings

In thermodynamics, we divide the universe into two parts:

**The system**, which refers to the specific part we're interested in studying (like the chemicals in a beaker during a reaction).

**The surroundings**, which refer to everything else around the system (the beaker, the air, the table, the room).

## Types of Systems

What's important is that systems and surroundings can exchange energy and sometimes matter. Systems can be classified based on how they interact with their surroundings:

**In open systems**, both energy and matter can freely move between the system and surroundings. Example: a boiling pot without a lid.

**In closed systems**, only energy can be exchanged, but matter stays locked inside. Example: a sealed water bottle that you heat up.

**Isolated systems** are the most restricted type—neither energy nor matter can get in or out. Example: a perfect thermos flask.

Now that we've looked at the basics of systems, let's explore the fundamental laws that govern how energy behaves. We'll start with the First Law of Thermodynamics.

## The First Law of Thermodynamics: Energy is Conserved

Here's the big idea: **Energy can neither be created nor destroyed; it can only be converted from one form to another.**

This is also called the Law of Conservation of Energy. When you burn wood, the chemical energy in the wood doesn't disappear—it transforms into heat and light energy. The total amount of energy remains the same.

In chemistry, we express this as:

**ΔU = q − w**

Here's what each symbol means:
- ΔU (Delta U) is the change in internal energy of the system
- q is the heat absorbed or released
- w is the work done by or on the system

This simply means that the change in a system's energy equals the heat it exchanges minus any work done by the system. So if a system absorbs heat, its internal energy increases; if it does work, its energy decreases by that amount.

Let's break down what heat and work actually mean in this context.

## Heat and Work: Two Ways Energy Moves

**Heat (or q)** stands for energy transferred because of a temperature difference. Heat always flows from hot to cold, like warmth from your hands melting an ice cube.

**Work (or w)** stands for energy transferred when a force moves something. In chemistry, this often means a gas expanding or contracting (like when a gas pushes a piston).

## Sign Conventions (Important!)

In thermodynamics, the direction of heat and work is important. By convention, work done by the system on surroundings is positive, and work done on the system is negative. The following sign conventions are used:

| Process | Sign of q | Sign of w | Effect on ΔU |
|---------|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| System absorbs heat | + | — | Increases |
| System releases heat | − | — | Decreases |
| Work done by the system (expansion) | — | + | Decreases |
| Work done on the system (compression) | — | − | Increases |

When the system absorbs heat, q is positive, and this increases the internal energy ΔU. When the system releases heat, q is negative, which decreases the internal energy. When work is done by the system, such as during expansion, w is positive, and the internal energy decreases. Finally, when work is done on the system, such as during compression, w is negative, and the internal energy increases.

Let's look at a simple example to make this clearer:

**Example 1: Gas Expansion**
For example, imagine that a gas absorbs 200 J of heat and does 50 J of work, like by expanding in a piston and pushing against the surroundings. This means that work is done by the system. Thus, w is positive. Let's calculate the change in internal energy:

ΔU = q − w = 200 − 50 = +150 J

→ The internal energy increases by 150 J.

Even though the system does work (which by itself would decrease internal energy), the heat absorbed is greater than the work done, so the net effect is an increase in internal energy.

Now let's talk about a concept that's especially useful for understanding chemical reactions in the lab: Enthalpy.

## Enthalpy: Heat Content at Constant Pressure

Most chemical reactions happen in open containers at constant atmospheric pressure (like in a beaker on your lab bench). For these conditions, we use a special term called **enthalpy (H)**.

At constant pressure, the heat absorbed or released by the system is equal to the change in enthalpy (ΔH = qₚ, where qₚ represents heat at constant pressure). The change in enthalpy (ΔH, or Delta H) tells us how much heat is absorbed or released during a reaction at constant pressure. There are two types of reactions.

**In exothermic reactions**, heat is released to surroundings (meaning that ΔH is negative). These reactions feel hot. Examples are burning wood or the rusting of iron.

**In endothermic reactions**, heat is absorbed from surroundings (meaning that ΔH is positive). These reactions feel cold. Examples are melting ice or evaporating water.

Now let's look at different types of enthalpy changes that chemists commonly measure.

## Types of Enthalpy Changes

In chemistry, we measure different kinds of enthalpy changes depending on the type of process occurring. These measurements are typically made under **standard conditions—298 K (or 25°C) and 1 atmosphere pressure**—and are indicated by the symbol °.

### Standard Enthalpy of Formation (ΔH_f°)

This is the enthalpy change when **one mole** of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states. If ΔH_f° is negative, the formation is exothermic (energy released); if positive, it's endothermic (energy absorbed). By convention, the standard enthalpy of formation of any element in its standard state is zero.

### Standard Enthalpy of Combustion (ΔH_c°)

This is the enthalpy change when **one mole** of a substance is completely burned in excess oxygen under standard conditions. Combustion reactions are always exothermic (negative ΔH), which is why burning fuels releases heat.

### Standard Enthalpy of Neutralization (ΔH_n°)

This is the enthalpy change when **one mole** of water is formed from the neutralization of an acid by a base under standard conditions.

### Standard Enthalpy of Solution (ΔH_sol°)

This is the enthalpy change when **one mole** of a substance is dissolved in a large excess of solvent (usually water) so that further dilution produces no additional heat change.

Understanding these different types of enthalpy changes is crucial, but how do we calculate enthalpy changes for reactions that are difficult to measure directly? That's where Hess's Law comes in.

## Hess's Law

Sometimes we want to know the enthalpy change for a reaction that's difficult or impossible to measure directly in the lab. **Hess's Law** provides a way to calculate this.

### The Law

**Hess's Law states**: The total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route taken. In other words, if a reaction can occur in multiple steps, the total enthalpy change is the sum of the enthalpy changes for each step.

This works because enthalpy is a **state function**—like internal energy (U), it depends only on the initial and final states, not on the path taken between them.

Think of it like climbing a mountain. Whether you take a direct steep path or a winding gentle path, your change in altitude from bottom to top is the same. Similarly, whether a reaction happens in one step or multiple steps, the total enthalpy change is the same. This is different for heat (q) and work (w), which are **path functions**, because they depend on how the process occurs. This distinction is fundamental to understanding thermodynamics.

### How to Use Hess's Law

To find the enthalpy change for a target reaction, identify known reactions with known enthalpy changes that can be combined. Manipulate these reactions (reverse them, multiply them) as needed, then add them together. Remember: reversing a reaction changes the sign of ΔH, and multiplying a reaction multiplies ΔH by that same factor.

### A Simple Example

Suppose we want to find the enthalpy change for C(s) + ½O₂(g) → CO(g), which is hard to measure directly. From known data for C → CO₂ (ΔH₁ = −394 kJ/mol) and CO → CO₂ (ΔH₂ = −283 kJ/mol), we can reverse the second equation and add:

- C(s) + O₂(g) → CO₂(g)   ΔH₁ = −394 kJ/mol
- CO₂(g) → CO(g) + ½O₂(g)   ΔH = +283 kJ/mol
- **Overall**: C(s) + ½O₂(g) → CO(g)   **ΔH = −111 kJ/mol**

This is the power of Hess's Law—we can calculate enthalpy changes for reactions we can't easily measure!

Now that we understand enthalpy changes, let's talk about how we actually measure the heat absorbed or released in chemical processes.

## Heat Capacity: How Much Heat Does It Take?

Have you ever noticed that water takes longer to heat up than oil? Or that a metal spoon gets hot much faster than a wooden spoon when placed in hot soup? This is because different substances have different abilities to absorb heat—we call this **heat capacity**.

### What is Heat Capacity?

**Heat capacity** is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of a substance by one degree Celsius (or one Kelvin).

There are two main types:

**Specific Heat Capacity (c)** is the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of **1 gram** of a substance by 1°C. Different materials have different specific heat capacities. Some examples are:
- Water has a high specific heat capacity (4.18 J/g°C) — it takes a lot of energy to heat up water
- Metals like iron have lower specific heat capacities (0.45 J/g°C) — they heat up quickly

**Molar Heat Capacity (C)** is the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of **1 mole** of a substance by 1°C. This is especially useful when dealing with chemical reactions.

### The Heat Equation

To calculate the heat absorbed or released when a substance changes temperature, we use:

**q = mcΔT**

Here's what each symbol means:
- q is the heat absorbed or released (in Joules)
- m is the mass of the substance (in grams)
- c is the specific heat capacity (in J/g°C)
- ΔT is the change in temperature (final temperature − initial temperature)

This equation allows us to calculate how much heat is needed to change the temperature of any substance.

## Calorimetry: Measuring Heat in the Lab

Scientists measure heat changes in chemical reactions using a technique called **calorimetry**. A calorimeter is an insulated container designed to measure heat changes during physical or chemical processes. When a reaction happens inside, any heat released or absorbed changes the temperature of the water in the calorimeter, allowing us to calculate the heat of the reaction.

In chemistry labs, a simple coffee cup calorimeter is used for reactions in solution, while a bomb calorimeter is used for combustion reactions.

The key principle is **conservation of energy**. In an insulated calorimeter, the heat released by the reaction equals the heat absorbed by the surroundings (usually water):

**−q(reaction) = q(water)**

Since q = mcΔT for the water, we can determine the heat of reaction by measuring the mass of water, its specific heat capacity, and the temperature change.

## Key Takeaways

Thermodynamics helps us understand energy changes in chemical reactions. The First Law tells us that energy is conserved—it can only be transformed from one form to another. We use concepts like internal energy, heat, work, and enthalpy to describe these energy changes, and understand whether reactions release heat (exothermic) or absorb heat (endothermic).

The beauty of thermodynamics is that it's a universal set of rules that governs all energy transformations in the universe!
